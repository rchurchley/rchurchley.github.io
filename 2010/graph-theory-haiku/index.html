Graph Theory Haiku

2010-11-22 Math

My most recent talk in UVic’s discrete math seminar presented three poetic proofs by Adrian Bondy… and three actual poems summarizing the ideas in each one.

Ore’s Theorem

A red-blue KnK_nKn​:
bluest Hamilton circuit
lies fully in GGG.

Ore’s theorem states:

Let GGG be a simple graph on n≥3n \geq 3n≥3 vertices such that d(u)+d(v)≥nd(u) + d(v) \geq nd(u)+d(v)≥n for any nonadjacent u,vu, vu,v. Then GGG contains a Hamilton cycle.

Bondy’s proof is roughly as follows. Colour the edges of GGG blue and add red edges to make a complete graph. The complete graph on n≥3n \geq 3n≥3 vertices has no shortage of Hamilton cycles, so choose one and label it v1v2…vnv1v_1 v_2 \ldots v_n v_1v1​v2​…vn​v1​.

Consider the blue neighbours of v1v_1v1​ on the cycle, and then move one vertex to the right along the cycle so you’re looking at the set

S={vi+1:v1vi∈E(G)} S = \{ v_{i+1}: v_1 v_i \in E(G) \} S={vi+1​:v1​vi​∈E(G)}

There are dG(v1)d_G(v_1)dG​(v1​) of these vertices, and if v1v2v_1 v_2v1​v2​ is red (i.e. v1v_1v1​ and v2v_2v2​ are nonadjacent in GGG), then the theorem’s hypothesis tells us that

∣S∣=dG(v1)≥n−dG(v2).|S| = d_G(v_1) \geq n - d_G(v_2). ∣S∣=dG​(v1​)≥n−dG​(v2​).

Since v2v_2v2​ has only n−1−dG(v2)n-1-d_G(v_2)n−1−dG​(v2​) red neighbours and is not itself in SSS, that means at least one vertex vi+1∈Sv_{i+1} \in Svi+1​∈S is a blue neighbour of v2v_2v2​. We can now replace v1v2v_1 v_2v1​v2​ and vivi+1v_i v_{i+1}vi​vi+1​ in the cycle with the two blue edges v1v2v_1 v_2v1​v2​ and v2vi+1v_2 v_{i+1}v2​vi+1​ to get a Hamilton cycle of KnK_nKn​ with at least one more blue edge than we had before.

We can make the same argument again and again until we have a Hamilton cycle of KnK_nKn​ with no red edges. The cycle being blue means it lies entirely in GGG, which proves the theorem.

Brooks’ Theorem

Greedily colour,
ensuring neighbours follow
all except the last.

Choose the last vertex wisely:
friend of few or of leaders.

Brooks’ Theorem says:

If GGG is connected and is not an odd cycle or a clique, then χ(G)≤Δ(G)\chi(G) \leq \Delta(G)χ(G)≤Δ(G).

If GGG is not regular, Bondy colours it as follows. Let rrr be a vertex of smaller-than-maximum degree. If we consider the vertices in the reverse order of a depth-first search rooted at rrr, each vertex other than rrr has at least one neighbour later in the order and at most d(v)−1d(v)-1d(v)−1 neighbours earlier. Greedily colouring in this order will assign one of the first d(v)≤Δ(v)d(v) \leq \Delta(v)d(v)≤Δ(v) colours to each v≠rv \neq rv=r, and at least one colour remains available for rrr since its degree is strictly smaller than Δ(G)\Delta(G)Δ(G).

If GGG is regular, there are a few cases. If it has a cut vertex, we can break the graph into two (non-regular) parts, colour each of them separately, and put them back together. If it has a depth-first tree that branches at some point, then Bondy constructs an ordering similar to the one for the non-regular case: if xxx has distinct children y,zy, zy,z in the tree, GGG can be ordered so yyy and zzz come first, xxx comes last, and every other vertex has at least one neighbour later on in the order. A greedy colouring according to this order uses at most Δ(G)\Delta(G)Δ(G) colours.

Finally, if GGG is regular, 2-connected, and all of its depth-first trees are paths, it turns out that GGG must be a chordless cycle, a clique, or a complete bipartite graph. Since bipartite graphs have χ(G)=2≤Δ(G)\chi(G) = 2 \leq \Delta(G)χ(G)=2≤Δ(G), the only exceptions to the theorem are odd cycles and cliques.

Vizing’s Theorem

Induction on nnn.
Swap available colours
and find SDR.

Vizing’s theorem says:

For any simple graph GGG, the edge chromatic number χ′(G)\chi'(G)χ′(G) is at most Δ(G)+1\Delta(G)+1Δ(G)+1.

The proof is by induction on the number of vertices; pick a vertex vvv and start with a (Δ(G)+1)(\Delta(G)+1)(Δ(G)+1)-edge-colouring of G−vG-vG−v.

Ideally, we could just colour the edges around vvv and extend the edge-colouring to one of GGG. Those colours would need to be a system of distinct representatives (SDR) for the sets of colours still available at each of the neighbours of vvv.

If we can’t find a full SDR, then we can still try to colour as many of vvv‘s edges as possible. Then, if an edge uvuvuv is still uncoloured, we can follow a proof of Hall’s Theorem to get a set of coloured edges u1v,u2v,…,ukvu_1 v, u_2 v, \ldots, u_k vu1​v,u2​v,…,uk​v such that:

  • uvuvuv can’t be coloured because there are fewer than k+1k+1k+1 total colours among those available at uuu and u1,u2,…,uku_1, u_2, \ldots, u_ku1​,u2​,…,uk​ in the colouring of G−vG-vG−v, but
  • if you un-colour any one of the edges and swap around the colours of the other ones, it would become possible to colour uvuvuv.

Note also that

  • regardless of how we assign Δ(G)+1\Delta(G)+1Δ(G)+1 colours to edges of GGG, each vertex will have at least one colour left unused by the edges around it.

The first and third facts put together tell us that some colour (say, blue) is available at two vertices among uuu and u1,u2,…,uku_1, u_2, \ldots, u_ku1​,u2​,…,uk​.

The second fact implies that any colour (e.g. red) still available at vvv can’t be available at any of the uiu_iui​, as otherwise we could swap around the colours to colour uvuvuv and then use red for uivu_i vui​v.

Look at the subgraph HHH of GGG formed by the red and blue edges — it’s made of paths and even cycles. We know that vvv is incident with a blue edge but not a red one, so it’s the end of a path in HHH.

We also know that there are two vertices among u,u1,u2,…,uku, u_1, u_2, \ldots, u_ku,u1​,u2​,…,uk​ that are incident with a red edge but not a blue one; those are also ends of paths in HHH. The paths might be the same as each other, and one might be the same as vvv‘s path, but the important thing is that we have a red-blue path where at least one of the ends is a neighbour u′u'u′ of vvv and the other end is any vertex other than vvv.

We can now modify the colouring of G−vG-vG−v to swap red and blue along this path, which frees up red at u′u'u′. If u′u'u′ is uuu itself, then we can colour uvuvuv red; otherwise, we can uncolour u′vu'vu′v, swap colours around to colour uvuvuv, and use red for u′vu'vu′v.

Either way, we’ve shown that the modified colouring of G−vG-vG−v has a larger set of distinct representatives than the original colouring. By repeating this process, we eventually find a colouring of G−vG-vG−v that has a full SDR — and therefore can be extended to a full (Δ(G)+1)(\Delta(G)+1)(Δ(G)+1)-edge-colouring of GGG.
